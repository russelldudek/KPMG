Dear KPMG Digital Nexus Hiring Team,

KPMG is at the point where AI innovation has to become an operating discipline. The firm is expanding across cloud and model ecosystems, building shared AI capabilities, and publicly describing the move from experimentation toward scale. The challenge behind this role, as I read it, is not simply to create more GenAI proofs of concept. It is to make experimentation cumulative: stronger problem framing, clearer human authority, better evidence, more realistic stress testing, and cleaner decisions about what to scale, reuse, partner, or stop.

That is the problem I have built my career around in different forms. At Vape-Jet, I lead AI-first operating transformation across production, quality, purchasing, customer success, support, engineering issue flow, enterprise systems, telemetry, and AI-assisted workflows in a robotics and machine-vision environment. Through DudeWorth, I develop AI-readiness and opportunity frameworks and agentic workflow patterns that connect context, retrieval, agents, human judgment, escalation, evaluation, and governance. At Amazon, I learned to build operating mechanisms inside a scaled, 24/7 customer-backward system. At Compunetics, I led engineering, manufacturing, quality, and technical programs where process control, root-cause discipline, and customer consequence were not optional.

I am not presenting myself as a career foundation-model researcher, and I will not claim platform-specific production ownership that my record does not support. My case is different: I have repeatedly worked at the boundary between emerging technology and operating reality—connecting engineers, users, controls, data, decisions, and adoption so promising technology becomes usable work.

My candidate thesis is simple: innovation compounds when every experiment leaves behind one of three assets—a reusable capability, reusable evidence, or a fast no. I have built a small interactive “GenAI Proving Ground” decision artifact to demonstrate how I would think about the role: frame the work, bound authority and risk, build the minimum enterprise proof, stress quality/economics/adoption, then compound the learning.

I would welcome the chance to test that thesis against KPMG's real environment, learn where the current system already works well, and discuss where my operating background could help Digital Nexus move faster without separating speed from trust.

Carpe diem,Russell Dudek
