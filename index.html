The mandate under the title

Convert GenAI experimentation into compounding enterprise capability.

The posting combines hands-on AI application development with POCs, technical leadership, external collaboration, roadmaps, and enterprise business impact. That makes the operating problem larger than model building alone: the role must improve how KPMG chooses, proves, and scales AI work.

Before a build begins

Five boundaries to make explicit.

01

Work boundary

Which decision, handoff, review, analysis, or client-facing workflow changes if this succeeds?

02

Authority boundary

What may the AI do, recommend, draft, retrieve, escalate, or never do without accountable human judgment?

03

Evidence boundary

What data, test set, edge case, and acceptance threshold will make the experiment decision-grade?

04

Economic boundary

What cost ceiling, latency tolerance, reuse assumption, and operating ownership make scale rational?

05

Adoption boundary

Whose behavior must change, what friction disappears, and what training or standard work makes the improvement stick?

Operating model

FRAME → BOUND → BUILD → STRESS → COMPOUND

A role-derived sequence for making experimentation cumulative. The outcome is not “more POCs.” It is stronger evidence, clearer decisions, reusable patterns, and earlier termination of weak ideas.

01

FRAME

Start with the work, the decision, the user, the baseline, and the business consequence—not the model.

02

BOUND

Set data access, human authority, risk envelope, expected evidence, and cost ceiling before engineering expands.

03

BUILD

Create the smallest enterprise-representative artifact capable of proving or disproving the core hypothesis.

04

STRESS

Test quality, failure modes, cost, latency, integration burden, and operator behavior under realistic conditions.

05

COMPOUND

Scale, partner, reuse, or retire. Preserve patterns, tests, lessons, and decision records for the next experiment.

Executive scorecard

Measure the system, not just the demo.

A balanced scorecard keeps technical performance connected to business, adoption, economics, controls, and learning speed.

Outcome

Business movement

Cycle time, decision quality, rework, capacity, revenue protection, or another workflow-specific result.

Use

Adoption behavior

Repeat usage in the target workflow, completion, handoff quality, override patterns, and user friction.

AI quality

Task performance

Accuracy, groundedness, failure modes, recovery behavior, latency, and domain-review outcomes.

Control

Trust and assurance

Review effectiveness, policy adherence, escalation quality, auditability, and human authority.

Economics

Cost and reuse

Run cost, implementation burden, support load, reuse potential, and marginal cost of the next deployment.

Learning

Decision speed

Time from idea to evidence, time to stop weak work, reuse of tests, and rate of resolved uncertainty.

First 90 days

Learn the system before prescribing the platform.

The entry plan begins with portfolio and workflow discovery, then tests the proving discipline on representative opportunities before institutionalizing reusable mechanisms.

Days 0–30

Map the proving system

  • Inventory representative demand, active experiments, platform paths, and decision owners.
  • Interview business, domain, engineering, data, risk, finance, and adoption stakeholders.
  • Trace several POCs from origin to present state; identify where evidence or ownership breaks.
Days 31–60

Test the discipline

  • Run two or three representative opportunities through the decision card and experiment contract.
  • Agree evaluation requirements for quality, control, cost, latency, and operator behavior.
  • Clarify scale, reuse, partner, hold, and stop decision rights.
Days 61–90

Make learning reusable

  • Publish a reusable experiment brief, evaluation pack, and decision record pattern.
  • Establish a lightweight cadence for evidence review and scale/stop decisions.
  • Capture common components, reusable tests, and recurring failure modes across experiments.

Hard objection

The strongest reasonable concern deserves a direct answer.

Hiring risk
“The posting asks for deep recent AI/ML experience and hands-on enterprise AI platform development. Is this candidate technical enough?”

The gap is real. The transferability argument is specific.

Russell's verified record supports AI workflow and agentic design, Python and TensorFlow exposure, AI-first operating transformation, technical program leadership, regulated autonomous systems, robotics and machine-vision operations, and high-reliability engineering. It does not justify claiming a five-year foundation-model research career or unverified production ownership of Azure AI Foundry or Vertex AI.

The case for fit is that the role's stated work is broader than research: enterprise POCs, cross-functional engineering coordination, platform and roadmap thinking, partner collaboration, resource prioritization, and business impact. Russell's differentiated edge is building the operating mechanisms around emerging technology so technical work reaches real users, real decisions, and measurable outcomes. Platform-specific depth should be tested directly in interview—not papered over.

Discovery protocol

Questions for the leaders doing the work.

These are designed to reveal the actual decision architecture behind the job.

Which GenAI experiments are currently stuck, and what is usually missing: data, trust, integration, economics, ownership, or user behavior?

Who has the final authority to scale, pause, partner, or stop an AI experiment—and what evidence do they trust?

Across Azure, Google, model, and partner ecosystems, what must be standardized and where does KPMG deliberately preserve optionality?

Which workflows are strategically important enough to justify custom AI capability rather than configuration of an existing platform?

How does Digital Nexus distinguish meaningful workflow adoption from tool usage or pilot participation?

What AI failure mode would be most damaging to trust with KPMG professionals or clients?

What artifacts from successful POCs are currently reusable by the next team, and which lessons are still trapped in individual projects?

Who owns AI unit economics after an experiment becomes an operating capability?
